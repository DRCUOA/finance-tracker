Getting Started

Finance Tracker helps you stay on top of your money by giving you a clear picture of your accounts, spending, and budgets. Here's the recommended order for setting things up.

  1. 1
    Create your accounts. Go to Accounts and add each bank account, credit card, savings account, or investment you want to track. Set the starting balance to the real balance on the day you begin.
  2. 2
    Review your categories. The app creates default categories when you sign up. Head to Categories to rename, delete, or add new ones. Set a monthly budget amount on the categories you want to track spending against.
  3. 3
    Add transactions. You can add them manually one at a time via Transactions, or if your bank supports Akahu, link it via Bank Feeds to sync them automatically.
  4. 4
    Set up matching rules. Go to Matching Rules and create keywords so future transactions are automatically suggested with the right category. For example, add the keyword "countdown" to your Groceries category.
  5. 5
    Explore your data. Visit the Dashboard for a high-level snapshot, Spending for a live "safe-to-spend" view, and Reports for detailed analytics.

Dashboard

The Dashboard is your home screen. It gives you a snapshot of your financial position at a glance.

Net Worth Cards

At the top you'll see your total assets, total liabilities, and net worth (assets minus liabilities). These are calculated from the current balances of all your accounts.

Period Summary

Below the net worth cards you'll see income and expenses for the current period (week or month). Use the arrow buttons to step forward and backward through periods. Switch between "week" and "month" view using the toggle.

Budget Progress

The budget card shows how much you've spent against your total budget for the period. Each category with a budget shows a progress bar. Categories in red have gone over budget.

Net Worth Trend Chart

The line chart shows how your net worth has changed over time. You can adjust the time horizon using the span buttons (6 months, 1 year, 5 years, or all time).

Term Filter

Use the term filter (Short, Medium, Long) to focus the dashboard on specific types of accounts. "Short" shows only short-term accounts (like everyday spending accounts). "Medium" includes short and medium-term. "Long" shows everything. This is useful if you want to see just your day-to-day financial position without investment or long-term savings accounts cluttering the view.

Recent Transactions

A quick list of your 10 most recent transactions appears at the bottom. Click any transaction to edit it.

Reconciliation Status

Each account card shows when it was last reconciled and whether there's a draft reconciliation in progress. This helps you spot accounts that haven't been checked against your bank statement recently.

Spending (Live Position)

The Spending page is your real-time "can I afford this?" view. It shows you how much you've spent so far this week or month and how much you have left before hitting your budget.

Spending Pulse

The hero section at the top shows your spending status with a colour-coded indicator. Green ("on track") means you're well within budget. Amber ("watch") means you're getting close. Red ("tight") means you're at or over your limit. This gives you an instant read on where you stand without needing to look at numbers.

Category Breakdown

Below the pulse, each spending category is listed with how much you've spent and your budget for the period. Click on any category row to expand it and see the individual transactions that make up that total.

Commitments

Commitments are planned expenses that haven't happened yet — things like upcoming bills, subscriptions, or one-off purchases you know are coming. They appear in amber within the category breakdowns so you can see the full picture of what's coming, not just what's already been spent. See the section for details.

Period Navigation

Use the left/right arrows to look at previous or future weeks/months. Toggle between weekly and monthly views to match how you think about your money.

Accounts

Accounts represent the real bank accounts, credit cards, and other financial accounts you want to track.

Creating an Account

Click "Add Account" and fill in the details. The key fields are:

  • Name — a label you'll recognise (e.g. "ANZ Everyday")
  • Type — Checking, Savings, Credit Card, Loan, Investment, or Cash. This determines whether the account is treated as an asset or a liability in your net worth.
  • Initial Balance — the balance on the day you start tracking. The app calculates the current balance by adding all your transactions to this starting number.
  • Term — Short (everyday accounts), Medium (savings you might dip into), or Long (investments, long-term savings). This controls the dashboard term filter.
  • Cashflow — leave this ticked for accounts that represent day-to-day income and spending. Untick it for investment or asset-tracking accounts you don't want included in your spending reports and budgets.

Asset vs Liability

The app automatically groups accounts based on their type. Checking, Savings, Investment, Cash, and Other are assets (money you have). Credit Card and Loan are liabilities (money you owe). Your net worth is assets minus liabilities.

Cashflow vs Asset-Only

When an account is marked "Asset Only" (cashflow unticked), it still counts toward your net worth but is excluded from spending reports, budget tracking, and the Spending page. This is useful for things like a KiwiSaver or investment account where the balance matters but the transactions aren't regular income or expenses.

Active vs Inactive

You can mark an account as inactive when editing it. Inactive accounts are hidden from most views but their data is kept. This is useful for closed accounts.

Balance Calculation

The current balance is always calculated as: initial balance + sum of all transactions. If you ever notice the balance is wrong, check that your initial balance is correct and that no transactions are missing or duplicated. The balance is recalculated automatically when you edit an account or finish a reconciliation.

Transactions

Transactions are the heart of the app — every dollar in and every dollar out is recorded here.

Adding a Transaction

Click "Add Transaction" and enter the date, amount, description, and account. Use a positive amount for money coming in (income, refunds) and a negative amount for money going out (purchases, bills). Choose a category to classify it, or leave it uncategorised and sort it later.

Duplicate Detection

When you add a transaction, the app checks if one already exists with the same account, date, amount, and description. If it finds a match, you'll see a warning. You can choose to save it anyway if it's genuinely a different transaction.

Category Suggestions

If you don't pick a category, the app will try to suggest one based on your matching rules (keywords in the transaction description). If it finds a match, it pre-selects that category for you. The more keywords you set up, the smarter this gets.

Searching and Filtering

Use the filter bar at the top to narrow down transactions by account, category, date range, or search text. You can also choose "Uncategorised" from the category dropdown to find transactions that still need a category. Results update instantly as you change filters.

Quick Editing

Click any transaction row to open a quick-edit modal where you can change the date, amount, description, account, category, reference, and notes without leaving the page. The modal also shows whether the transaction is locked (reconciled) or editable.

Batch Operations

Select multiple transactions using the checkboxes, then use batch actions to categorise them all at once or delete them in bulk. There's also a "Select All" option that selects every transaction matching your current filters, not just the ones visible on the current page.

Review Uncategorised

The "Review Uncategorised" button shows you all transactions without a category, along with suggested categories based on your matching rules. You can accept or change the suggestions and apply them in one go. This is the fastest way to clean up a batch of new transactions.

Locked Transactions

Transactions that have been reconciled (or are part of a draft reconciliation) are locked. A red padlock icon appears next to them. You can't edit or delete locked transactions — this protects the integrity of your reconciled balances. If you need to change a reconciled transaction, you'll need to deal with the reconciliation first.

Categories

Categories organise your transactions into meaningful groups so you can see where your money goes. Think of them as labels like "Groceries", "Rent", "Salary", or "Entertainment".

Category Types

Each category has a type: Income (money coming in — salary, interest), Expense (money going out — food, bills), or Transfer (moving money between your own accounts). Transfers are excluded from income and expense totals in reports so they don't inflate your numbers.

Parent and Child Categories

Categories can be organised in a parent/child hierarchy. For example, you might have a parent "Food & Drink" with children "Groceries", "Restaurants", and "Coffee". This helps you see both the detail and the big picture in reports.

Monthly Budgets

Set a monthly budget on any category to track how much you plan to spend. This default budget appears in the Dashboard and Reports. If you need a different budget for a specific month (e.g. December when spending increases), you can set a month-specific override — the app will use the override for that month and the default for all others.

Fixed vs Flexible

Mark a category as "Fixed cost" if the amount is roughly the same every period (like rent or insurance). Leave it unchecked for flexible spending (like groceries or entertainment). The Reports page has a "Fixed vs Flexible" breakdown that uses this flag to help you understand which parts of your spending are locked in and which parts you have control over.

Keywords

You can attach keywords to a category directly from the Categories page. Keywords are words or phrases that appear in transaction descriptions. When a keyword matches, the app suggests that category for the transaction. For example, add "pak n save" to your Groceries category and any transaction with that phrase will be suggested as Groceries.

Merging Categories

If you end up with two categories that should be one, you can merge them. All transactions from the source category are moved to the target category, and the source is deleted. This is a one-way operation — make sure you're merging in the right direction.

Matching Rules

Matching Rules is a dedicated page for managing the keywords that power automatic category suggestions. It's the same keyword system as on the Categories page, but presented in a way that makes it easier to manage rules across all categories at once.

How Matching Works

When a transaction comes in (either manually created or via bank feed), the app checks its description against all your keywords. The match is case-insensitive — "COUNTDOWN" matches a keyword of "countdown". If a keyword phrase appears anywhere in the description, that category is suggested.

Creating Rules

To add a rule, type a keyword phrase and select the category it should match to. The page shows you how many uncategorised transactions currently match that phrase, so you can see the impact before saving.

Uncategorised Preview

The bottom section shows all your uncategorised transactions. Click any transaction description to copy it — this makes it easy to create a keyword from an actual transaction. The preview count updates as you type a new keyword phrase.

Rule Health

The health check highlights potential problems with your rules: duplicate keywords (the same phrase on multiple categories), stale rules (keywords that have never matched anything), and overly broad rules (very short keywords that might match too many things). Cleaning these up improves the accuracy of auto-categorisation.

Hit Counts

Each rule shows a hit count — how many times it has been used to categorise a transaction. Rules with high hit counts are your most valuable. Rules with zero hits might need their keyword phrase adjusted or might simply be for a merchant you haven't visited yet.

Reconciliation

Reconciliation is the process of checking that the transactions in the app match your actual bank statement. It's how you make sure nothing is missing or wrong.

How to Reconcile

  1. Go to the Reconcile page and pick the account you want to reconcile.
  2. Enter the statement date and ending balance from your bank statement.
  3. The app shows all uncleared transactions on or before that date. Tick the ones that appear on your bank statement.
  4. As you tick transactions, the running "cleared balance" updates. When it matches your statement balance, everything lines up.
  5. Click Finish to lock those transactions as reconciled.

Draft Reconciliations

If you can't finish in one sitting, click "Save Draft". Your checked selections are saved and you can come back later to pick up where you left off. While a draft is active, the transactions in it are locked from editing to prevent changes that would throw off your reconciliation.

Discarding a Draft

If you started a reconciliation and want to start over, you can discard the draft. This unlocks the transactions and removes your saved selections.

What Happens When You Finish

When you finish a reconciliation, the selected transactions are marked as "cleared" and permanently locked. The account balance is recalculated. A record is kept of the reconciliation date and balance for your history.

Reconciliation History

Each account has a reconciliation history page showing all past reconciliations with their statement dates and balances. This is useful for auditing and checking when an account was last verified.

If the Balance Doesn't Match

If your cleared balance doesn't match the statement balance after ticking everything, it usually means a transaction is missing, duplicated, or has the wrong amount. Check for transactions that appear on the statement but not in the app, and vice versa.

Reports

Reports give you detailed analytics about your money over time. There are multiple tabs, each showing a different angle on your finances.

Income vs Spending

Shows your total income alongside your total spending for the selected period, plus a trend chart showing how these have changed over recent periods. This is useful for seeing whether you're consistently spending more than you earn, or whether you're building up savings.

Budget vs Actual

Lists every expense category with a budget, showing how much you planned to spend versus how much you actually spent. Categories over budget are highlighted. When viewing a weekly period, the monthly budget is prorated (divided by approximately 4.3) to give you a weekly target.

Spending by Category

A breakdown of all your spending grouped by category with a comparison to the average over recent periods. This helps you spot categories where you're spending more or less than usual. Click any category to drill down into the individual transactions.

Fixed vs Flexible

Splits your spending into fixed costs (rent, insurance, subscriptions — categories you've marked as "fixed") and flexible costs (everything else). This shows you how much of your spending is locked in versus how much you have discretion over. If you're trying to cut costs, focus on reducing the flexible portion.

Cash Flow Trend

A 12-period chart showing income and spending side by side over time. This is the best view for spotting long-term patterns — are expenses creeping up? Is income seasonal?

Category Deep Dive

The deep dive page lets you select a specific category and see its spending history in detail, including a chart and a full transaction list. Use this when you want to understand exactly where money is going within a single category over time.

Transfers in Reports

Transactions categorised as "Transfer" are excluded from income and expense totals. This prevents money moved between your own accounts from inflating your spending or income figures.

Bank Feeds (Akahu)

Bank Feeds let you sync transactions directly from your bank using Akahu, a New Zealand open banking platform. Instead of manually adding every transaction, you can pull them in automatically.

Setting Up

To use bank feeds, you need an Akahu account. Sign up at my.akahu.nz and connect your bank. Then add your Akahu App Token and User Token to the app's environment configuration. Once configured, the Bank Feeds page will show your connected bank accounts.

Linking Accounts

Link each Akahu bank account to a local account in Finance Tracker. The link is one-to-one — each Akahu account can only be linked to one local account. Once linked, you can sync transactions and balances.

Syncing Balances

Click "Sync Balances" to update all linked account balances from Akahu in one go. This updates the current balance on each account to match what the bank reports.

Syncing Transactions

For each linked account, you can sync transactions for a date range. Choose a start and end date and the app will pull in transactions from Akahu. Transactions are checked for duplicates (by Akahu transaction ID) so syncing the same range twice won't create duplicates. The app also tries to auto-categorise synced transactions using your matching rules.

Unlinking

You can unlink an account at any time. Unlinking just removes the connection — it doesn't delete any transactions that were already synced.

Backup & Restore

The Backup page lets you export your data for safekeeping and restore it if needed.

Full Backup

Downloads everything — accounts, categories, keywords, transactions, budgets, and commitments — as a single JSON file. This is the best option for a complete safety copy. Do this regularly.

Restoring a Backup

Upload a previously exported JSON backup file to replace all your data. This is destructive — it overwrites everything. Always make sure you have a current backup before restoring an older one.

Table Exports

Export individual tables (transactions or accounts) as CSV or JSON. CSV is useful for opening in a spreadsheet. JSON is useful for programmatic use or transfer.

Account Bundles

Export selected accounts as a portable bundle. You can export just the schema (account settings) or include all transactions. This is useful for sharing account structures or moving accounts between instances of the app.

Matching Rules & Categories

You can export and import matching rules and category structures separately. Importing matching rules merges them with existing rules (duplicates are skipped). Importing categories replaces all existing categories — transactions are remapped by category name where possible, but unmatched ones become uncategorised.

◊ Understanding Confidence Levels

Not all future expenses are equally certain. Confidence levels let you express how sure you are about each commitment:

Confirmed

Definitely happening. You've signed the contract, booked the ticket, or it's a known fixed bill.

"My rent is $1,200 on the 1st. No question."
Expected

Very likely to happen. You're fairly sure about the amount and timing, but it's not locked in yet.

"My power bill is usually around $150."
Tentative

Might happen. You're considering it, or it depends on other factors. Great for "planning ahead" scenarios.

"I might need new tyres this quarter — maybe $600?"

⪢ Quick Reference

Adding a Commitment

From the Spending page, add a commitment with a title, amount, due date, and direction (outflow for expenses, inflow for income). Optionally assign it to a category and set a confidence level.

Recurring Commitments

Tick "recurring" and set a recurrence pattern (like "monthly" or "fortnightly") to have the commitment automatically projected into future periods. The app creates future instances so you can see expected bills in upcoming months without re-entering them.

Clearing a Commitment

When the expected expense or income actually happens (and you've added the real transaction), clear the commitment to mark it as done. This removes it from the pending view — the "accrual" is released because the real impact is now known.

Tips & Good Habits

A few things that will help you get the most out of Finance Tracker.

Categorise Everything

The reports and budgets only work well if your transactions are categorised. Make it a habit to review uncategorised transactions regularly. The "Review Uncategorised" feature on the Transactions page makes this quick.

Build Up Your Keywords

Every time you categorise a transaction, think about whether the merchant or description should be a keyword. The more keywords you add, the fewer transactions you'll need to categorise manually in the future. Check the Matching Rules health report to keep your rules clean.

Reconcile Regularly

Reconciling monthly (or even weekly for busy accounts) catches missing transactions and data-entry errors early, before they compound. The Dashboard shows reconciliation status per account so you can see which ones need attention.

Set Realistic Budgets

Use the "Spending by Category" report with averages to see what you actually spend before setting budgets. A budget that's too tight will just be ignored; one based on real spending gives you a useful target.

Use Commitments for Bills

Add your regular bills (rent, insurance, subscriptions) as recurring commitments. This way the Spending page shows you not just what you've spent, but what's still coming, giving you a real "safe-to-spend" number.

Take Backups

Download a full backup regularly. It takes seconds and gives you peace of mind. Keep a few copies — if something goes wrong, you can restore from the latest backup.

Use the Right Account Types

Getting account types right matters because it affects net worth calculations. A credit card should be "Credit Card" (liability), not "Checking" (asset). A home loan should be "Loan". If your net worth looks wrong, check your account types first.

Dark Mode

Click the sun/moon icon at the bottom of the sidebar to toggle between light and dark mode. Your preference is remembered across sessions.
